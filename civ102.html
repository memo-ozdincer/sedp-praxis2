CIV102 Matboard Bridge Design

Optimizing Structural Efficiency Through Computational Analysis and Iterative Design

Project Summary

The CIV102 Engineering Mechanics and Design course challenged teams to design and construct a 1200mm span box girder bridge from matboard (max 0.825m²) to maximize structural efficiency (failure load / material area) under a moving train load. This required balancing multiple competing failure modes.

Our team (Team 706, including myself, Ella Mochoruk, and Ghazala Singh) embraced an analysis-driven approach. I developed a custom Python-based structural analysis tool (Engineering Analysis Framework) to rapidly evaluate iterations. This tool calculated section properties, generated SFD/BMDs for the moving load, and assessed failure modes like material stress, shear, plate buckling, and stability. This aligned with my principles of analytical precision and systems thinking.

Through systematic iteration guided by the analysis, we evolved the design significantly. We used implicit SCAMPER-like modifications (SCAMPER) - modifying height and web spacing, eliminating unnecessary flange material, and rearranging diaphragm placement. The final design featured variable web spacing, optimized diaphragms, and selective reinforcement, achieving a predicted Factor of Safety (FOS) of 1.20 (controlled by Flange Tip Buckling) while using only 45.6% of the allowed material.

Construction involved precise techniques and collaboration. Load testing (Prototype Testing) validated our analysis, with failure at 538.41N (99.3% of prediction) in the expected mode. The project highlighted the power of computational analysis for optimization and the critical link between theoretical design and practical construction realities.

1. The Structural Challenge & Requirements

Maximize load capacity / material area for a 1200mm matboard bridge under a moving load, using ≤ 0.825m² material and only white glue. This required balancing strength against multiple failure modes within strict constraints.

CTMF Applied: Requirements Development (#2)

Competition rules were translated into quantitative requirements (Span, Material Area, Maximize Load/Area) and constraints (Material Properties: Tensile=30MPa, Compressive=6MPa, Shear=4MPa, E=4GPa).

Assessment: Clear quantitative targets were essential for the analysis-driven optimization. Defining material properties as constraints was critical for accurate modeling.

2. Computational Structural Analysis

A custom Python tool automated analysis: calculating section properties, generating SFD/BMDs for the moving load, and assessing multiple failure modes (material stress, shear, plate buckling, stability). This allowed rapid iteration and optimization.

The analysis revealed the critical load case and highlighted the interplay between failure modes – optimizing one often made another the limiting factor, necessitating a balanced approach.

CTMF Applied: Engineering Analysis Framework (#9)

The custom Python tool integrated calculations for section properties, load analysis, and multiple failure modes. This allowed rapid, quantitative evaluation of design iterations against requirements, enabling evidence-based optimization.

Assessment: Cornerstone of the project's success. Enabled systematic optimization beyond manual calculation capabilities. Allowed precise balancing of competing failure modes. Embodied analytical precision and systems thinking.

3. Design Evolution & Final Design

Guided by analysis, the design evolved iteratively: increasing height, optimizing web spacing, removing non-critical bottom flange material, adding optimized diaphragms, and reinforcing the top flange.

CTMF Applied: SCAMPER Technique (#5) (Implicitly/Retrospectively)

The iterative refinement process implicitly used SCAMPER principles: Modifying dimensions, Eliminating non-critical material, Rearranging component spacing. Formal application might have structured this exploration.

Assessment: The project succeeded through analysis-driven iteration, which aligns with SCAMPER's goal of systematic variation. Formal SCAMPER prompts could be useful in similar optimization tasks to ensure all modification avenues are considered.

4. Construction Process

Construction demanded precision. Key techniques included optimizing material layout for cutting, using jigs for alignment, laminating flanges with staggered joints, and a modified folding technique for diaphragm stability. Collaboration was key: I focused on analysis, Ghazala Singh on material optimization, and Ella Mochoruk on construction techniques.

5. Testing Results & Reflection

Load testing validated the analysis: failure occurred at 400 (143 N lower than expected) via Flange Tip Buckling at the predicted location. This demonstrated that, while the code and raw calculations may suggest one thing, real life scenarios are much more complex, and hard to account for.

CTMF Applied: Prototype Testing Framework (#11)

The official competition load testing served as the validation test. The standardized procedure allowed direct comparison against predicted performance.

Assessment: The close match between prediction and reality (99.3% accuracy on failure load, correct mode/location) provided strong validation for the Engineering Analysis Framework (#9) used. It offered definitive empirical evidence of the design's success.

CTMF Applied: Pugh Chart Analysis (#7) (Implicitly/Retrospectively)

The iterative design process implicitly compared variations against the previous design (baseline) using criteria like FOS, material use, and constructability, aligning with Pugh logic.

Assessment: Formal Pugh Charts could have potentially streamlined early iterations by making comparisons more explicit, possibly reducing the number of full analysis cycles needed. Useful for structuring comparisons in optimization tasks.

Reflection

This project was a powerful exercise in applying analytical precision and systems thinking to a constrained optimization problem. Developing the custom analysis tool was essential for navigating the complex interplay of failure modes. It reinforced the idea that optimization requires a holistic view, as improving one area often shifts the bottleneck. The discrepancy between theoretical perfection and construction realities (glue strength, alignment precision) was a key learning, emphasizing the need to integrate constructability into early design analysis. The project's success validated the analysis-driven iterative design approach.
