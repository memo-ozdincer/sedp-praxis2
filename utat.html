UTAT Servo Control Board

Eliminating Jitter Through Precision Signal Integrity and Power Optimization

Project Summary

As part of the University of Toronto Aerospace Team's (UTAT) rocketry division, I addressed persistent servo jitter issues compromising flight control reliability. The servos we were using exhibited position instability (1-2° oscillations) despite using standard hardware, posing a risk to flight stability. The challenge was to identify the root cause and redesign the servo control board to ensure precise, jitter-free operation under demanding flight conditions (0-70°C, high EMI).

My approach involved systematic analysis (Problem Decomposition) of potential causes including signal timing, power delivery, and EMI. Detailed signal integrity analysis (Engineering Analysis) using oscilloscopes and LTSpice simulation revealed that power supply instability during servo activation, not PWM timing, was the primary cause. This deep focus on the electrical behavior was key. Based on this evidence, I redesigned the PCB layout, focusing on power distribution (wider traces, star topology), enhanced filtering (LC networks), improved grounding, and higher reliability connectors (Weighted Decision Matrix used for component selection). This work was done in collaboration with teammate Markiyan Konyk, who focused on the schematic design.

The redesigned board was fabricated, assembled, and rigorously tested (Prototype Testing). Testing confirmed the complete elimination of servo jitter, achieving position stability within ±0.1°. Power supply voltage droop under load was reduced below 50mV. The project successfully enhanced flight control reliability and provided valuable design guidelines for future UTAT projects. Schematic to lay out the board and to communicate the board's layout to members not directly involved in the project (Technical Documentation) captured the analysis, design rationale, and results. 1. Problem Definition & Initial Investigation

UTAT's rocketry flight control system experienced unreliable servo positioning (jitter), posing a significant risk. My initial step involved understanding the scope and potential causes, applying my 'Exposure & Familiarity First' principle by examining existing hardware and reported issues before diving into theory.

CTMF Applied: Problem Decomposition (#3)

The problem was decomposed into potential root causes: Signal Integrity, Power Supply, EMI Susceptibility, and Thermal Considerations. This systematic approach prevented premature focus on timing issues and ensured all critical aspects were investigated.

Assessment: This structured breakdown was crucial. It made the complex problem manageable and directly led to investigating the power delivery aspect, which proved to be the root cause.

2. Signal & Power Analysis (Deep Focus)

Applying my 'Deep Focus' principle, I conducted detailed analysis. PWM signal timing analysis confirmed stability, ruling out timing as the primary cause. Oscilloscope measurements then revealed significant power supply voltage fluctuations during servo activation, correlating with the jitter. This phase required hours of focused examination of the board and signals.

LTSpice simulations modeled the power distribution network, confirming the voltage droop hypothesis and allowing rapid virtual testing of different filtering solutions before physical implementation.

CTMF Applied: Engineering Analysis Framework (#9)

Combined empirical oscilloscope measurements with predictive LTSpice simulation. Measurements validated simulation accuracy (8-22% deviation), increasing confidence in using simulation to evaluate filter designs efficiently.

Assessment: Essential for identifying the root cause (power instability) and exploring solutions efficiently. Simulation significantly reduced prototyping needs for filter designs. Balancing simulation with measurement was key for model validation and aligns with my evidence-based approach.

3. PCB Design Optimization

The analysis directly informed the PCB redesign, focusing on power integrity and reliability. Key changes included widened power traces, star-topology distribution, enhanced LC filtering, improved grounding, high-reliability screw terminals, a temperature-compensated oscillator, and better thermal management. This addressed the identified weaknesses systematically.

Collaboration: I led the PCB layout and analysis, while teammate Markiyan Konyk focused on the schematic design. We used Altium Designer for collaboration and held regular reviews with the UTAT Rocketry Avionics team.

CTMF Applied: Weighted Decision Matrix (#8)

Used to quantitatively evaluate component choices (e.g., connectors, filter components) against weighted criteria like reliability, performance, cost. Justified selecting screw terminals due to high reliability weighting for flight systems.

Assessment: Made trade-offs explicit and justifiable, aligning with evidence-based decisions. Crucial for prioritizing reliability in a safety-critical application.

4. Results & Documentation

The redesigned board successfully eliminated servo jitter, providing reliable and precise control critical for UTAT's flight systems. Performance metrics confirmed significant improvements in stability and accuracy.

CTMF Applied: Technical Documentation Framework (#10)

Created detailed reports including analysis, design rationale, simulation results, test protocols, and performance data. Used annotated schematics and layout diagrams. Structured documentation for accessibility by team members with varying expertise.

Assessment: Essential for knowledge preservation and transfer within UTAT. Layering information and using visuals improved communication. Integrating documentation throughout the process was more effective than treating it as a final step. Addressed communication adaptability growth area.

Reflection

This project strongly validated my design philosophy emphasizing deep focus and analytical precision. The systematic decomposition and evidence-based analysis were key to diagnosing a non-obvious root cause. The iterative process, combining simulation and rigorous testing, led to a robust solution. It highlighted the importance of power integrity in complex electronic systems and provided practical experience in high-reliability PCB design. Documenting the process ensured the lessons learned benefited the wider UTAT team.
