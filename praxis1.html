Praxis I: Dryer Performance Assessment

Developing a User-Centered Solution for Chestnut Residence Laundry Facilities

Project Summary

The Praxis I project tackled user frustration with inconsistent dryer performance at Chestnut Residence. Residents wasted time and money re-running cycles due to dryers not reaching effective temperatures. The goal was to create a simple, reliable, low-cost method for residents and staff to verify dryer heating function.

Our team prioritized understanding user needs through stakeholder analysis (Stakeholder Analysis) with residents and staff. Key requirements (Requirements Development) emerged: simplicity, reusability (>50 cycles), clear binary indication (working/not working around 60-70°C), and safety.

Divergent ideation (Lotus Blossom) explored electronic, chemical, mechanical, and phase-change concepts. Concept evaluation (Pugh Chart, WDM) favored a phase-change material (wax) solution for its inherent binary indication, simplicity, and low cost.

The final recommended design was a durable, two-chamber device: one chamber holding indicator wax formulated to melt at the target threshold, the other a reference material. Prototype testing (Prototype Testing) validated the concept's feasibility, melting point accuracy, and durability. The project concluded with a design proposal and presentation (User-Focused Presentation).

1. Problem Framing & Stakeholder Needs

Chestnut Residence dryers performed inconsistently, causing resident frustration (wasted time/money) and diagnostic challenges for maintenance. We needed a simple way to check if dryers reached effective temperatures.

Key insights from stakeholders (residents, staff, maintenance): need for simple binary indication, low cost, reusability, safety, and speed for maintenance checks.

CTMF Applied: Stakeholder Analysis (#1)

Identified diverse needs: residents wanted simplicity/low cost; maintenance needed speed/reliability. Prioritizing these shaped requirements.

Assessment: Crucial for defining the need for a simple binary indicator, steering away from complex sensors. Direct engagement was key.

CTMF Applied: Requirements Development (#2)

Translated needs into measurable specs: Binary result at ~60-70°C, cost <$5, >50 cycles durability, non-toxic, quick check time.

Assessment: Provided concrete targets (esp. temperature, cost) essential for evaluating concepts effectively.

2. Concept Generation & Evaluation

We explored concepts using brainstorming and Lotus Blossom: electronic sensors, chemical strips, bimetallic strips, phase-change materials (wax, alloys).

Evaluation via Pugh charts and a WDM favored the phase-change (wax) concept due to simplicity, low cost, inherent binary indication, and reusability potential.

CTMF Applied: Lotus Blossom Method (#6)

Used to expand on "Dryer Temp Indication," branching into Electronic, Chemical, Mechanical, Phase Change categories, ensuring diverse approaches were considered.

Assessment: Structured brainstorming, prevented early fixation on electronic solutions, led to considering phase-change materials.

CTMF Applied: Pugh Chart (#7) & WDM (#8)

Pugh chart screened options; WDM selected wax concept based on high weighting for cost, ease of use, and clarity.

Assessment: Effective combination. WDM provided quantitative justification aligning with prioritized stakeholder needs (simplicity, low cost).

3. Detailed Design & Prototyping

The design featured a two-chamber device: Chamber 1 with indicator wax (melting ~65°C), Chamber 2 with a reference material. Prototyping involved testing waxes, designing/3D printing heat-resistant casings (PETG/ABS), and validating performance in ovens and dryers.

CTMF Applied: Prototype Testing Framework (#11)

Tests validated melting accuracy, casing durability (>50 cycles), and indication clarity post-cooling. Used controlled oven tests and real-world dryer tests.

Assessment: Essential for confirming feasibility and refining the wax formulation and casing. Revealed practical challenges (e.g., wax clarity after re-solidifying) leading to design adjustments.

4. Final Recommendation & Reflection

The final recommendation was the two-chamber wax indicator device, meeting key requirements: low cost, ease of use, clear binary indication, safety, and reusability.

Reflection

This project underscored the value of user-centered design (Stakeholder Analysis) – understanding the *real* need led to a simple, effective solution over a potentially complex one. The iterative process of ideation, evaluation (WDM), and prototyping (Prototype Testing) was key. My analysis of Praxis I also revealed that tackling difficult sub-problems ('tangents'), like finding the right wax and ensuring indication clarity, often yielded the most critical insights, reinforcing my 'Embracing Discomfort' principle. Communicating this simple solution required clear framing (User-Focused Presentation) to highlight its effectiveness despite its simplicity.
