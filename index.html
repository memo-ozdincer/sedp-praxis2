Engineering Design Portfolio

Documenting an Evolving Design Practice

Welcome to my Student Engineer Design Portfolio, created for ESC102 Praxis II. This portfolio serves as both a showcase of my design work since September 2024 and a personal handbook documenting my evolving engineering design philosophy and the methodologies (CTMFs) I utilize.

It aims to demonstrate how I have changed as an engineer engaging in engineering design in class, my ability to express designs and arguments effectively, and my experiences working within design teams. This document reflects on key projects—UTAT Servo Control, CIV102 Bridge, Praxis I Dryer Assessment, and Praxis II LEGO Sorter—using them as evidence for my design position and CTMF assessments. It is intended to be a useful reference for myself in future design activities and to provide insight into my design practice for the teaching team and potential collaborators.

My Engineering Design Philosophy

Introduction: Evolving Through Experience

My approach to engineering design is fundamentally shaped by practical experience and reflection, rather than purely theoretical ideals. It's an evolving philosophy, grounded in the projects I've completed (UTAT, CIV102, Praxis I, Praxis II) and the conscious and subconscious patterns identified through them. I believe the most effective solutions to complex problems often emerge from a blend of creativity and painstaking effort, which usually reveals itself as simple, elegant, and easily communicable designs.

This statement represents an iteration on my original position from January 2024. Experiences since then, particularly the deep technical dive in UTAT and the stakeholder-centric challenges in Praxis II, have refined my understanding. Key evolutions include a stronger emphasis on systematic problem decomposition early on, a greater appreciation for quantifiable evidence in decision-making (even for qualitative factors), and recognizing the crucial role of clear documentation not just as a final step, but as an integral part of the design process itself. This portfolio is structured to reflect this philosophy, using project evidence to validate my position and assess the tools I employ.

Core Values & Principles

My design practice is guided by:

  • Methodical Progression: A structured, linear-first approach minimizes wasted effort, especially when tackling a problem initially.
  • Deep Focus: Intense concentration on specific, often difficult, aspects yields insights that frequently benefit the entire project (UTAT Jitter Analysis).
  • Embracing Discomfort: Actively seeking out challenging areas or knowledge gaps accelerates learning and often leads to the most significant breakthroughs (Praxis I Tangents).
  • Exposure & Familiarity First: Gaining initial broad exposure to concepts, even superficially, builds a foundation for faster, deeper understanding later (UTAT Initial Learning).
  • Simplicity & Clarity: Striving for solutions that are fundamentally simple and can be communicated clearly and effectively (Praxis I Indicator).
  • Evidence-Based Decisions: Grounding choices in quantifiable metrics and structured analysis whenever possible (CIV102 Analysis, WDM).

My Design Process Model (Iterated)

My process, inspired by FDCR but emphasizing initial linearity and deep focus, follows these steps:

  1. Idea Flow (Foundation/Familiarity): Initial exposure and mental mapping without judgment. (FDCR Frame).
    Connection: Learning UTAT circuits via schematics before theory.
  2. Embracing Discomfort (Catalyst): Identify and prioritize the hardest parts or knowledge gaps.
    Connection: Praxis I finding that breakthroughs came from tackling tangents.
  3. Deep Focus (Implicit Brainstorming): Intense, distraction-free work on a narrow scope.
    Connection: Hours spent on the UTAT servo board led to identifying the signal pin current issue. See Fig 2 below.
  4. Communication & Representation (Reality Check): Documenting and sharing for feedback. (FDCR Represent, but informs all).
    Connection: Praxis I report synthesis; this portfolio itself.

After an initial pass, I revisit steps as needed, allowing for non-linear iteration informed by the first structured exploration.

Concepts, Tools, Models & Frameworks (CTMFs)

This section details the key CTMFs I utilize, organized by the Frame•Diverge•Converge•Represent (FDCR) model. Each entry includes a description, evidence of use from my projects, and a critical assessment of its utility and limitations, reflecting on its contribution to my design practice and connection to my philosophy.

Frame CTMFs

Tools for understanding context, defining requirements, and structuring the design space.

1. Stakeholder Analysis

Systematic identification and prioritization of individuals/groups affected by a design, mapping their interest and influence.

Application: Used in Praxis II (LEGO Sorter) to understand ToroLUG needs, revealing accuracy was paramount over speed.

Assessment:

Crucial for defining the *right* problem. Provides structure but requires direct engagement for nuances. Helps prioritize requirements effectively, aligning with evidence-based decisions. Connects to Systems Thinking by mapping the ecosystem. Useful early and when re-scoping.

Lessons: Revisit throughout; combine with direct interviews.

2. Requirements Development

Translating stakeholder needs into specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, time-bound (SMART) design specifications.

Application: Defined structural/material limits in CIV102 Bridge. Set accuracy/speed targets in Praxis II, refined iteratively based on feedback.

Assessment:

Provides essential clarity, focus, and objective evaluation criteria. Supports Specificity of Purpose. Needs flexibility for evolution; rigid adherence can stifle innovation. Measurable criteria are key for evidence-based design.

Lessons: Treat as living document; link explicitly to validation tests.

Diverge CTMFs

Techniques for generating diverse potential solutions and exploring the design space creatively.

4. "Would Be Nice" (WBN) Method

Structured brainstorming encouraging exploration beyond core requirements by considering ideal features without immediate constraints.

Application: Used in Praxis II (LEGO Sorter) to explore features like self-cleaning, leading to prioritization of color detection.

Assessment:

Excellent for expanding thinking and identifying innovations. Helps navigate ambiguity. Requires rigorous prioritization (value vs. complexity) to prevent scope creep. Separates ideation from evaluation effectively.

Lessons: Use quantitative criteria in prioritization phase.

5. SCAMPER Technique

Uses prompts (Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify, Put to another use, Eliminate, Reverse) to stimulate creative variations on existing designs.

Application: Implicitly used in CIV102 Bridge refinement (Modify web spacing, Eliminate flange parts). Used in Praxis II (Adapt industrial feeders).

Assessment:

Systematic way to explore variations and overcome fixation. Best for refining existing concepts. Complements analytical precision by providing structured creativity. Requires a starting point.

Lessons: Combine with analysis to validate generated ideas.

6. Lotus Blossom Method

Visual ideation expanding concepts outward from a central theme via related sub-themes in a grid structure.

Application: Used in Praxis II (LEGO Sorter) to explore sorting mechanisms (vision, mechanical, sensor-based) from the core "LEGO Sorting" theme.

Assessment:

Provides visual structure for systematic, broad exploration while maintaining focus. Good for team ideation. Helps identify connections and hybrid opportunities. Can become complex if over-layered.

Lessons: Ground primary themes in research; document cross-theme links.

Converge CTMFs

Methods for evaluating, comparing, and selecting promising solutions based on evidence and criteria.

7. Pugh Chart Analysis

Compares alternatives against criteria relative to a baseline concept (+, -, S scoring). Useful for initial filtering and identifying hybrid opportunities.

Application: Used in Praxis II (LEGO Sorter) to compare feeding mechanisms, leading to a hybrid design.

Assessment:

Efficient for early filtering and concept synthesis. Highlights specific strengths/weaknesses. Doesn't account for criteria weighting, making it less suitable for final selection alone. Facilitates team discussion.

Lessons: Best used before WDM; consider criteria weighting separately.

8. Adjusted Comparison Matrix]

Due to the complex nature of this project, we decided to make an offshoot of the comparison amtrix that was supposed to mainly be helpful for seeing which requirements were hit best, and it ended up being really helpful in seeing how our prototypes compared to eachother (qualitatively)

Application: Used in UTAT Servo to evaluate power distribution designs, prioritizing voltage stability. Used in Praxis I for final concept selection, used in Praxis II as described above. Figure shows Praxis 2 implementation

Assessment:

Strongly supports evidence-based decisions by quantifying trade-offs. Makes rationale transparent. Requires careful, potentially subjective, weight assignment. Numerical precision can imply false objectivity if inputs aren't robust.

Lessons: Determine weights collaboratively; perform sensitivity analysis.

Represent CTMFs

Frameworks for documenting, communicating, and demonstrating design solutions effectively.

10. Technical Documentation Framework

Structured approach to recording and communicating design info (specs, rationale, visuals) accessibly for relevant stakeholders.

Application: Used in UTAT Servo reports. Applied in Praxis II annotated bibliography.

Assessment:

Crucial for knowledge preservation, communication, and traceability. Visuals and layering enhance accessibility. Most effective when integrated throughout the process, not just done at the end. Addresses communication adaptability growth area.

Lessons: Integrate early; use visual techniques heavily.

11. Prototype Testing Framework

Systematic methodology for evaluating prototypes against requirements using defined protocols, controlled variables, and success criteria.

Application: Developed proxy tests in Praxis II (LEGO Sorter). Used comprehensive protocols in UTAT Servo. Load testing in CIV102 Bridge.

Assessment:

Provides essential empirical validation and structured feedback for iteration. Identifies unforeseen issues. Connects directly to evidence-based design. Can be resource-intensive but yields high value in de-risking designs.

Lessons: Involve stakeholders in test planning; test early and often.

12. User-Focused Presentation Framework

Structuring communication of technical info for non-technical stakeholders, emphasizing user value/context via accessible narratives and visuals.

Application: Used in Praxis II (LEGO Sorter) presentation to ToroLUG. Used in CIV102 Bridge visuals for evaluators.

Assessment:

Effective for bridging communication gaps and gaining stakeholder buy-in. Addresses communication adaptability growth area. Requires effort to translate technical details accurately but accessibly. Visuals and analogies are key.

Lessons: Understand audience deeply; use interactive elements.

References

The following references informed the design principles, methodologies, and technical approaches presented in this portfolio.

Standards & Guidelines

International Organization for Standardization (ISO). (2018). ISO 9241-11: Ergonomics of human-system interaction – Part 11: Usability: Definitions and concepts. Retrieved from ISO 9241-11

This part defines usability as the extent to which a product can be used by specified users to achieve specified goals with effectiveness, efficiency, and satisfaction in a specified context of use. It emphasizes that usability is an interaction outcome influenced by the user, task, equipment, and environment. This standard informed the user-centered aspects of the Praxis projects, ensuring focus on effectiveness and satisfaction for target users (residents, ToroLUG members).

International Organization for Standardization (ISO). (2019). ISO 9241-210: Ergonomics of human-system interaction – Part 210: Human-centred design for interactive systems. Retrieved from ISO 9241-210

Provides requirements and recommendations for a human-centred design process, emphasizing understanding users, specifying requirements, developing design solutions, and evaluating them iteratively. This standard directly guided the overall process followed in both Praxis I and Praxis II, particularly the emphasis on stakeholder engagement and iterative prototyping based on user feedback.

Nielsen, J. (2020). 10 Usability Heuristics for User Interface Design. Nielsen Norman Group. Retrieved from NN/g Heuristics

A set of ten general principles for interaction design serving as practical rules-of-thumb. Heuristics like "Visibility of system status," "User control and freedom," and "Error prevention" were considered during the UI design phase of the Praxis II LEGO sorter and the indicator design in Praxis I, aiming for an intuitive user experience.

International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC). (2018). IEC 60812: Analysis techniques for system reliability - Procedure for failure mode and effects analysis (FMEA). Retrieved from IEC 60812

Details procedures for Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA). Although a formal FMEA wasn't conducted in these projects, the underlying principle of systematically identifying potential failure points informed the analysis in the CIV102 bridge project (considering multiple structural failure modes) and the UTAT servo project (troubleshooting jitter causes).

Professional Engineers Ontario. (n.d.). Code of Ethics. Regulation 941/90. Retrieved from PEO Code of Ethics

Sets out the primary duty of professional engineers to public welfare, and principles of fidelity, competence, and fairness. This code underpins the ethical considerations in all projects, particularly regarding safety (CIV102, UTAT) and giving credit to teammates (Req 9).

University of Toronto. (n.d.). Code of Behaviour on Academic Matters. Retrieved from UofT Academic Code

Defines academic integrity standards. Relevant for ensuring proper citation and giving credit for collaborative work within team projects, as required by portfolio Req 9.

Design Methodologies

Cross, N. (2008). Engineering Design Methods: Strategies for Product Design (4th ed.). Wiley.

A foundational text providing a comprehensive overview of systematic engineering design methods. Concepts like functional decomposition, morphological charts, and objective evaluation methods discussed in this book informed the selection and application of various CTMFs used in my projects (e.g., #3, #6, #8).

Wang, L., Shen, W., Xie, H., Neelamkavil, J., & Pardasani, A. (2002). Design for manufacturability and design for "X": Concepts, applications, and perspectives. The International Journal of Advanced Manufacturing Technology, 19(4), 326-334.

Discusses the DfX philosophy, emphasizing consideration of lifecycle aspects during design. This perspective influenced thinking about constructability in the CIV102 bridge project and usability/maintainability in the Praxis projects.

Pugh, S. (1991). Total Design: Integrated Methods for Successful Product Engineering. Addison-Wesley.

Introduces the Pugh Method (Pugh Chart, CTMF #7) for concept selection. This book provided the theoretical basis for using this method in Praxis II for initial concept screening and identifying hybrid opportunities based on comparative strengths and weaknesses relative to a datum.

Serrat, O. (2017). The SCAMPER Technique. In Knowledge Solutions (pp. 311-314). Springer, Singapore.

Provides a detailed explanation of the SCAMPER technique (CTMF #5). This reference informed the structured application (even if implicit) of modification prompts during the refinement phases of the CIV102 bridge and Praxis II feeder mechanism designs.

Ulrich, K. T., & Eppinger, S. D. (2016). Product Design and Development (6th ed.). McGraw-Hill Education.

A widely used textbook covering the product development process. Concepts related to requirements definition, concept generation, prototyping, and testing discussed here provided broader context for the methods applied in Praxis I and II.

Dym, C. L., Agogino, A. M., Eris, O., Frey, D. D., & Leifer, L. J. (2005). Engineering Design Thinking, Teaching, and Learning. Journal of Engineering Education, 94(1), 103-120.

Discusses the cognitive processes involved in engineering design thinking. This paper informed my reflection on my own design process model, particularly the interplay between structured methods and implicit brainstorming during deep focus.
